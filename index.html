Our Centennial

Where precision
meets purpose.

Stanford Surgery traces its roots to 1908, when Stanford took over Cooper Medical College in San Francisco, and to the formal founding of its surgical division in 1914. Under Emile Holman's leadership, the department built an identity rooted in academic rigor, innovation, and deep commitment to training.

Through a century of firsts — the first West Coast kidney transplant, the first U.S. adult heart transplant, the CyberKnife, and the first intestinal spring lengthening device — Stanford Surgery has led medicine from the operating room. The Holman 100 centennial gathers our community to honor that legacy and launch the next century with the Holman 1-10-100 Fundraising Pledge.

Stanford Surgery Milestones — History Walk

World firsts.

The centennial history walk documents the landmark procedures and inventions that define Stanford Surgery's unmatched record of precedent-setting breakthroughs.

Panel 1
1959
First Kidney Transplant in the Western United States
Performed by Roy Cohn and Samuel Kountz — the first West Coast transplant of its kind.
Panel 2
1961
First Kidney Transplant Between Non-Twins
Performed by Samuel Kountz while still a resident, rewriting the limits of transplant immunology.
Panel 3
1968
First Successful Adult Heart Transplant in the U.S.
Performed by Dr. Norman Shumway, the culmination of a decade of rigorous cardiac surgery research at Stanford.
Panel 4
1981
First Successful Heart-Lung Transplant in the World
Stanford leads the world in transplantation once again, opening a new era for pulmonary and cardiac patients.
Panel 5
1992
First Thoracic Aortic Endograft in the United States
A milestone that celebrated its 25th anniversary in 2017. Dr. Lee's original patient remains alive and well.
Panel 6
1994
First Robotic, Image-Guided Radiosurgery (CyberKnife)
Invented by Stanford neurosurgeon Dr. John Adler, the CyberKnife revolutionized precision radiation therapy — eliminating the need for invasive skeletal fixation frames.
Panel 7
1999
First FDA-Approved Abdominal Aortic Endografts
The Aneurx and Zenith systems — developed and commercialized by Stanford surgeon teams — transform vascular care.
Panel 8
2004–05
First Robotic Roux-en-Y Gastric Bypass
Developed and documented by Stanford surgeons as a safe, totally robotic technique — transforming bariatric surgery.
Panel 9
2005
First Robotic Kasai Procedure
A landmark achievement in pediatric robotic surgery, opening new possibilities for the youngest patients.
Panel 10
2023
First Intestinal Spring Lengthening Device in Humans
FDA approval granted for the first human trials of a device developed by Dr. James Dunn to treat short bowel syndrome. Dr. Joseph Forrester placed the first device in a human patient.

What We Stand For

The pillars of
Stanford Surgery.

I
Surgical Excellence

From Emile Holman to Norman Shumway to today's faculty, Stanford Surgery has held every surgeon to an uncompromising standard — technically rigorous, ethically grounded, always patient-first.

II
Education & Training

The residency legacy of Halsted runs through Stanford's halls. With integrated plastics, vascular, and cardiovascular programs — and partners at PAVA, SCVMC, and Kaiser since 1961 — we train surgeons who change the world.

III
Discovery & Innovation

From the first heart-lung transplant to the CyberKnife to the intestinal spring device, Stanford Surgery's laboratories have produced a century of breakthroughs — and we are not finished.

IV
Global Surgery & Equity

The first integrated Plastics residency with a global surgery track reflects our conviction that surgical excellence must reach every patient — regardless of geography, income, or background.
