Our Centennial

Where precision
meets purpose.

Stanford Surgery traces its roots to 1908, when Stanford took over Cooper Medical College in San Francisco, and to the formal founding of its surgical division in 1926. Under Emile Holman's leadership, the department built an identity rooted in academic rigor, innovation, and deep commitment to training.

Through a century of firsts — the first West Coast kidney transplant, the first U.S. adult heart transplant, the CyberKnife, and the first intestinal spring lengthening device — Stanford Surgery has led medicine from the operating room. The Holman 100 centennial gathers our community to honor that legacy and launch the next century with the Holman 1-10-100 Fundraising Pledge.

Stanford Surgery Milestones — History Walk

World firsts.

The centennial history walk documents the landmark procedures and inventions that define Stanford Surgery's unmatched record of precedent-setting breakthroughs.

Panel 1
1926
Founding of the Department of Surgery
Emile Holman, trained under the legendary surgeon William Halsted at Johns Hopkins, became Stanford's first Chair of Surgery in 1926 and held the role for nearly three decades until 1955. During his tenure he established the surgical residency program, founded the Halsted Laboratory of Surgical Research, and built the academic infrastructure that would define Stanford Surgery for generations. He became internationally recognized for his research on arteriovenous fistulas, publishing more than 40 papers on the subject, and later served as president of the American Association for Thoracic Surgery.
Panel 2
1954
First Open-Heart Surgery on the West Coast
Frank Gerbode performed the first open-heart operation on the West Coast in 1954, correcting an atrial septal defect. This placed Stanford at the frontier of cardiac surgery at a time when open-heart procedures were extraordinarily rare anywhere in the world, and set the stage for the cardiac surgery program that would go on to define the institution.
Panel 3
1959
First Kidney Transplant in the Western U.S.
Roy Cohn, one of the world's pioneers in organ transplantation, performed the first kidney transplant on the West Coast alongside Samuel Kountz, then a surgical resident at Stanford. Kountz had come to Stanford from the University of Arkansas specifically to study transplantation, and this operation ignited a career that would reshape the entire field. The West Coast had no precedent for this procedure, making Stanford the leading transplant center west of the Mississippi from its very first attempt.
Panel 4
1961
First Kidney Transplant Between Non-Twins
Still a resident at Stanford, Kountz made history by performing the first successful kidney transplant between two people who were not identical twins. Before this, transplantation was thought to be feasible only between twins because of their near-identical genetics — the immune system would reject any other donor organ. Kountz's success shattered that assumption and opened the door to transplantation between unrelated individuals, which is now the standard. He went on to perform more than 500 kidney transplants in his career, more than any physician in the world at the time of his death.
Panel 5
1968
First Adult Heart Transplant in the U.S.
On January 6, 1968, Norman Shumway performed the first adult human heart transplant in the United States on 54-year-old steelworker Mike Kasperak. The operation was the culmination of nearly a decade of research in which Shumway and his team — working largely with canine models — perfected the surgical technique, developed the method of preserving donor hearts in cold saltwater, and studied the mechanisms of organ rejection. When most of the world abandoned heart transplantation in the early 1970s due to poor survival rates, Shumway kept going. His persistence, and the innovations his team made in immunosuppression and rejection monitoring, ultimately made heart transplantation a viable and widespread procedure.
Panel 6
1972
First Cardiac Biopsy on a Transplant Patient
One of the central challenges of early heart transplantation was detecting organ rejection before it was too late. Previously, surgeons could only identify rejection through EKG abnormalities — by which point intervention was often futile. In 1972, Stanford's Edward Stinson and visiting scientist Philip Caves developed the first cardiac biopsy technique for transplant patients, using a tiny catheter to sample heart tissue directly. This gave clinicians an early warning system for rejection, dramatically improving survival rates and reducing the need for heavy immunosuppressive drugs. It remains a cornerstone of transplant monitoring to this day.
Panel 7
1981
First Heart-Lung Transplant in the World
Building on the immunosuppression advances of the late 1970s — particularly the adoption of cyclosporine — Bruce Reitz and Norman Shumway performed the world's first successful combined heart-lung transplant in 1981. The patient was Mary Gohlke, a 45-year-old advertising executive with end-stage pulmonary hypertension. She lived five more years and wrote a book about her experience. The operation opened an entirely new frontier for patients with combined cardiac and pulmonary failure, for whom no treatment had previously existed.
Panel 8
1984
First LVAD Implant
Stanford researchers developed a left ventricular assist device (LVAD) — a mechanical pump that takes over the work of a failing heart's main pumping chamber. In 1984, Stanford's Phil Oyer implanted one for the first time, using it to keep a gravely ill patient alive. The LVAD has since become one of the most important tools in the treatment of advanced heart failure, used both as a bridge to transplant and, increasingly, as a long-term destination therapy for patients who are not transplant candidates.
Panel 9
1992
First Thoracic Aortic Endograft in the U.S.
On July 20, 1992, Stanford's Michael Dake, D. Craig Miller, and R. Scott Mitchell implanted the first aortic endograft in the United States — a hand-built, self-expanding stent-graft placed inside the diseased aorta via a catheter rather than open surgery. This was a transformative moment in vascular surgery: where previously a thoracic aortic aneurysm required a massive open operation with enormous risk, the endovascular approach offered a far less invasive alternative. Stanford pioneered the technique for both thoracic aneurysms and aortic dissections, and it is now the standard of care worldwide.
Panel 10
1994
First Robotic Image-Guided Radiosurgery / CyberKnife
Stanford neurosurgeon John Adler invented the CyberKnife — a robotic system that delivers precisely targeted beams of radiation to tumors anywhere in the body, guided in real time by imaging. First used clinically in 1994, the CyberKnife eliminated the need for the rigid, invasive skeletal fixation frames that had previously been required to hold a patient still during radiosurgery. It could treat tumors in the brain, spine, lung, liver, and elsewhere with sub-millimeter accuracy, and without a single incision. The technology has since been used to treat hundreds of thousands of patients worldwide.
Panel 11
1995
First Heartport Minimally Invasive Heart Surgery
In 1995, Bruce Reitz performed the first Heartport procedure at Stanford — a technique that allows surgeons to perform open-heart surgery through small incisions between the ribs, bypassing the need to saw open the sternum. The approach dramatically reduced trauma, blood loss, pain, and recovery time for cardiac patients. Stanford played a central role in developing and commercializing the Heartport system, which became a major advance in making cardiac surgery accessible to a broader population of patients.
Panel 12
2004
First Use of Berlin Heart for Pediatric Patients in the U.S.
In 2004, Lucile Packard Children's Hospital Stanford became one of the first hospitals in the United States to use the Berlin Heart EXCOR — an external mechanical heart pump designed for children — keeping a 3-month-old infant alive for 55 days until a donor heart became available. At the time, no good mechanical bridge-to-transplant option existed for small children, and the Berlin Heart filled a critical gap. Stanford's early adoption and experience with the device helped establish the evidence base for its eventual FDA approval and widespread clinical use.

What We Stand For

The pillars of
Stanford Surgery.

I
Surgical Excellence

From Emile Holman to Norman Shumway to today's faculty, Stanford Surgery has held every surgeon to an uncompromising standard — technically rigorous, ethically grounded, always patient-first.

II
Education & Training

The residency legacy of Halsted runs through Stanford's halls. With integrated plastics, vascular, and cardiovascular programs — and partners at PAVA, SCVMC, and Kaiser since 1961 — we train surgeons who change the world.

III
Discovery & Innovation

From the first heart-lung transplant to the CyberKnife to the intestinal spring device, Stanford Surgery's laboratories have produced a century of breakthroughs — and we are not finished.

IV
Global Surgery & Equity

The first integrated Plastics residency with a global surgery track reflects our conviction that surgical excellence must reach every patient — regardless of geography, income, or background.
